🌿 I've never played a TTRPG

Imagine four people around a table. One of them — the Game Master (GM) — describes a world: a rain-soaked town, a locked door, a nervous stranger. The other three describe characters who live in that world. When something uncertain happens ("I try to pick the lock"), you roll dice. The dice don't tell you what happens — they tell you how well it goes. Everything else is a conversation.

That's a tabletop RPG. No board. No winning. Just collaborative storytelling with dice to keep it honest.

What is Fate Condensed?

Fate is one of many RPG systems. What makes Fate special: it cares more about who your character is than what they can do. Your character is described by short phrases called aspects — like "Retired Detective Who Can't Stop Noticing Things". Those phrases are always true in the story, and they're also the engine that drives the game.

What you need to start

  • Four Fate dice (or a free dice app)
  • Some tokens (pennies, poker chips — anything small)
  • Index cards for notes
  • One person willing to be the GM
  • Ogma handles the rest — it generates characters, scenes, and adventures so the GM doesn't prep from scratch
🎪 What a session looks like

The GM describes a scene: "You're in the back room of a diner. The lights are dim. A woman you've never met is sitting at your usual table." You decide what your character does: "I sit down across from her and say nothing." The GM decides if that needs a roll: "She's sizing you up — roll Rapport to see if you make a good impression." You roll, add your skill number, and the result tells you how it goes. A session lasts 2–3 hours. Most of it is talking and deciding. Dice come out maybe 15–20 times.

⚔ I play D&D

You already know what a TTRPG is. You understand skills, dice, and turn-based conflict. That foundation transfers. But Fate Condensed is a fundamentally different kind of game, and the habits that make you a good D&D player will actively mislead you here. The shift isn't mechanical — it's philosophical.

The three things that trip up D&D players most

1. You describe first, then pick the mechanic. In D&D you say "I roll Athletics." In Fate you say "I sprint across the rooftop and leap for the fire escape" — and the GM decides which skill fits. Fiction drives mechanics, not the other way around.

2. Failure is never boring. A missed attack in D&D means nothing happens. In Fate, the GM offers "success at a cost" — you succeed, but something goes wrong. The story always moves forward.

3. Getting in trouble makes you stronger. In D&D, you minimise bad outcomes. In Fate, you accept complications (compels) to earn fate points — the currency you spend to be awesome later. Embracing your character's flaws is the optimal strategy.

🎲 I play other RPGs

You know what a GM is, what dice do, what a skill check means. Here's what makes Fate Condensed different from whatever system you're coming from, in five concepts:

The Aspect Economy. Every important truth is an aspect — a short phrase. Character aspects, situation aspects, consequences. They can be invoked (spend a fate point for +2 or a reroll), compelled (accept a complication, earn a fate point), and they're always narratively true. If the room is Pitch Black, you cannot see. This is the core innovation — everything else flows from it.

Four Actions, Four Outcomes. Overcome (clear an obstacle), Create Advantage (add aspects to the scene), Attack (harm someone), Defend (resist). Each produces Fail / Tie / Succeed / Succeed with Style. No action economy, no bonus actions, no reactions.

Fiction First. Describe what you're doing. The table decides which skill and action fit. Then roll. If you're coming from PbtA, this is familiar. If you're coming from GURPS, it's a significant shift.

Stress Is Not Hit Points. Stress boxes are binary (checked/unchecked), each absorbs 1 shift, and they clear every scene. They're plot armor, not health. Consequences (Mild=2, Moderate=4, Severe=6) are the real injuries — they're aspects that persist and can be invoked against you.

The Fate Point Cycle. Start with Refresh (default 3). Spend to invoke aspects. Earn by accepting compels. The rhythm: accept trouble now → be awesome later.

🎭 Help me run Fate better

You understand the rules. You've read the book. Now you need to make it work at the table.

Scene framing. Every scene needs a question. "Can the party get past the guards?" "Will the informant trust them?" If you can't state it in one sentence, the scene isn't focused enough. When the question is answered — the scene is over. Cut immediately.

Setting difficulty. Don't look up a chart. Ask: is this hard for this character? If it's routine, don't roll. If it's tough, set opposition at or slightly above their skill. The adjective ladder is a calibration tool, not a lookup table.

The compel rhythm. If the story feels flat, you're not compelling enough. Every PC's Trouble should trigger at least once per session. If you're not handing out fate points, the players can't spend them, and the whole economy stalls. Compels are the accelerator pedal.

Fail forward, always. "Nothing happens" is never the result of a failed roll. Success at a cost is your best friend. "You pick the lock, but the alarm was on a separate circuit." Every failure changes the situation.

The Create Advantage cascade. The first time a player stacks three free invokes and drops a +10 hit, they'll never attack-every-round again. Don't explain it — engineer the first encounter so it happens naturally. Give them an enemy who's clearly too strong to hit directly.

NPC shorthand. Minor NPCs: one aspect, one skill, 0–2 stress. Major NPCs: full aspects, skill pyramid, consequences. Spend prep time on majors and let Ogma handle the minors.
