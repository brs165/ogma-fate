D&D Transition

Coming from D&D 5e

You already know how to play TTRPGs. Here's every major mechanical and philosophical difference between D&D 5e and Fate Condensed — section by section, with concrete play examples.

📚 Where this fits

This is the deep-dive reference. For a quick overview of the three biggest shifts, see New to Ogma? → I play D&D. For Fate mechanics without D&D comparison, see Fate Mechanics.

>The big philosophical shift

D&D asks: "What can your character do?" — and answers it with a class, subclass, ability scores, proficiencies, and a grid of features.

Fate asks: "Who is your character?" — and answers it with five short phrases (aspects) that are always true, a skill pyramid, and three stunts. Everything else is narrated, not tracked.

This matters because it changes what you optimise for. In D&D, you build around mechanical capability. In Fate, you build around narrative interest — a character is interesting because of their flaws, not despite them.

Characters: Aspects vs. ability scores

D&DFate Condensed
6 ability scores (STR, DEX, CON, INT, WIS, CHA)5 aspects — short phrases, not numbers
Class + subclass = identityHigh Concept aspect = identity ("Burned-Out Fixer With One Last Job")
Background provides flavour, minimal mechanicsTrouble aspect drives drama ("My Loyalty Will Be My Ruin")
Proficiency in specific skills and toolsOne skill pyramid (peak at +4, no negatives possible)
Feats are optional mechanical upgradesStunts are +2 bonuses in specific circumstances or once-per-scene effects
🎪 Example — D&D character vs Fate character

D&D: Human Rogue 5, STR 10 DEX 18 CON 12 INT 14 WIS 13 CHA 16. Expertise in Stealth and Sleight of Hand. Uncanny Dodge. Evasion.

Fate: High Concept: "Chrome-Fingered Pickpocket Who Knows Every Face in the Market." Trouble: "I Owe the Syndicate a Debt I Can't Pay." Stunt: "When I palm a small object in a crowd, I get +2 to Stealth."

The Fate version tells you who she is and what will get her in trouble. The D&D version tells you what she can do. Both are valid — they're answering different questions.

Skills: Broad strokes, not specifics

D&D has 18 skills. Fate Condensed has 19 skills — but they're dramatically broader. Athletics covers everything D&D's Acrobatics, Athletics, and some Survival tasks do. Notice covers Perception and some Investigation. There are no tool proficiencies, no weapon proficiencies, no saving throw proficiencies.

The skill pyramid means every character is deliberately incompetent at most things. This is by design — the incompetence creates drama and supports character specialisation without requiring a 20-level ability progression.

The four actions — not attack/bonus/move/reaction

Fate actionWhat it doesD&D equivalent (approximate)
OvercomeClear an obstacle, remove a situation aspectAbility check against a DC
Create AdvantageAdd/discover an aspect, gain free invokesHas no real equivalent — this is Fate's superpower
AttackInflict stress shifts on a targetAttack roll → damage roll
DefendResist an attack or advantageArmour Class / saving throw

The most important action D&D doesn't have is Create Advantage. It places a new aspect on a target, an ally, or the scene. That aspect has a free invoke — anyone can cash it for +2 on a relevant roll. Stacking two or three free invokes before the decisive attack is Fate's version of "pre-buffing." It's the tactically optimal play that most D&D converts discover in their second session.

Aspects: The heart of the system

Every aspect is a phrase that is always narratively true. If the room is Pitch Black, you cannot see — no roll to perceive light that isn't there. If an NPC has the aspect Loyal to the Old King, you can compel that to complicate their relationship with the new regime.

Aspects are mechanically active in two ways:

  • Invoke — spend a fate point (or use a free invoke) to get +2 or a reroll. You must explain how the aspect is relevant.
  • Compel — the GM offers a fate point in exchange for the aspect making your life harder. Refuse by spending a fate point. Accept by taking the fate point and the complication.

Fate Points: The economy of drama

In D&D, inspiration is a binary bonus you either have or don't. In Fate, fate points are a flowing economy.

  • You start each session at your Refresh (default 3).
  • You spend fate points to invoke aspects (be more awesome now).
  • You earn fate points by accepting compels (accept trouble now, be awesome later).
  • At zero fate points, you cannot refuse compels — the story bends toward your trouble aspects.

The optimal play is not to conserve fate points — it's to spend freely and accept compels to refill. GMs who understand this hand out compels constantly and the economy hums.

Combat: Stress, Consequences, and Taken Out

D&DFate Condensed
Hit points — persistent across encounters, healed by rest or spellsStress boxes — clear every scene, not cumulative
Damage is abstract (HP lost), specific effects via conditionsShifts are absorbed by stress or paid with consequences
Death saving throws — knocked out at 0 HPTaken Out — attacker narrates what happens to you
Healing word, potions, short/long restConcede before being Taken Out — lose but narrate how on your terms
⚠ Stress is NOT hit points

Stress boxes each absorb exactly 1 shift and clear at the end of every scene. There is no "5 HP remaining" equivalent. A character who took 3 stress boxes in scene 1 is fully reset in scene 2. The persistent damage track is the consequence slots — not stress.

Initiative: Popcorn style

D&D uses fixed initiative order (roll dex, act in sequence). Fate Condensed uses popcorn initiative: after you act, you choose who goes next — ally or enemy. There is no fixed turn order.

This creates tactical decisions about pacing. Enemies can chain activations. Players can chain activations. The group that controls the sequencing controls the tempo of the conflict.

Challenges, Contests, and Conflicts

In D&D, almost all dramatic situations resolve as combat. Fate Condensed has three structured scene types:

  • Challenge — a series of overcome rolls against different obstacles. Success on all → success; failure on some → success at a cost.
  • Contest — two sides racing toward a goal. Each exchange, both roll; higher effort scores a victory. First to 3 victories wins.
  • Conflict — the full combat structure, when someone wants to harm someone else and the other side resists.

Quick FAQ

Where's the damage? How much does a sword do? In Fate, weapons don't deal fixed damage — you roll your Fight skill, and shifts above the defender's effort become stress. A sword might grant +1 Weapon rating (add +1 to shifts on successful attacks), but there's no d6/d8/d10 damage die.

Can my character die? Yes, but only if you agree. The standard outcome of being Taken Out is narrative removal from the conflict — captured, fled, dramatically incapacitated. Permanent death is a table conversation, not a mechanical outcome.

There's no CR. How do I know if an encounter is appropriate? You don't balance by math — you balance by stakes. A fight where the GM doesn't know who will win is a fight worth having. Most encounters in Fate are significantly harder than "appropriate-by-CR," because consequences create lasting complications even from victories.

What about magic? There's no spell list. Magic is handled by aspects and stunts. "I am a Flame-Touched Witch" as a High Concept means you can invoke it to justify fire-related attacks and creates advantages. A stunt might say "once per session, I can cause a fire to erupt at any visible point within 30 feet."
