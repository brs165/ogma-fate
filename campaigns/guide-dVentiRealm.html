⚔ dVenti Realm

The Senate fell. The dragons followed. The world is still learning what that means.

The Arcane Senate collapsed thirty years ago and left a legal vacuum that guilds, temples, and liches have been fighting over ever since. Eighteen months ago, the last known great wyrm died — and the world is only now understanding what dragons were actually for. Their territorial claims kept ancient things sealed underground. Their hoards grounded wild magic. Their presence suppressed what lived in the deep places. All of that is gone now. The dungeons are still there. The contracts are still binding. The things that were being contained are not.

Tone & Atmosphere

Two collapses, one world. The Senate's fall created a legal and institutional void — expired licences, unrenewed compacts, constructs running thirty-year-old directives, a phylactery registry nobody has the authority to open. The dragons' extinction created an ecological one — ward-lines failing, deep creatures surfacing, kobold clans in existential crisis, ancient things stirring under mountains that used to be safe.

The game lives in the gap between bureaucratic procedure and genuine dread. Every session is shaped by two questions: what's waking up now, and who's trying to fill the gap. The contracts are real. The monsters are also real. The guilds are trying to solve the second problem with tools designed for the first, and it is not going well.

For Players

You are dungeon delvers, guild contractors, void-touched mercenaries, kobold crew bosses, and scholars of things the Senate classified and forgot to unclassify. You navigate a world where every job has a contract, every contract has a penalty clause, and the thing the contract was written to contain may no longer be in the container.

Your class is your High Concept. Your spell is a stunt. Your dungeon licence covers you for the first three floors. What's on the fourth floor now is a different question entirely.

Coming from D&D?

You already know what dragons are. The question this world asks is: what were they for? Everything else transfers. Kobolds, golems, undead, aberrations from the deep places, liches with phylacteries and lawyers — the vocabulary is the same. The system underneath is leaner. No action economy. No hit points. Your Sneak Attack is a stunt. Your Counterspell is a stunt. Your Bardic Inspiration is a stunt — all 28 are named after D&D class features and translated directly to Fate mechanics.

The biggest shift: in D&D, the dragon is the encounter. In dVenti Realm, the dragon was the infrastructure. You're not here to fight what the dragons left behind. You're here to survive it, negotiate with it, or figure out how to do what the dragons were doing — without being dragons.

Key Factions

The Guild Network — contracts, licences, arbitration, and an increasingly desperate attempt to apply commercial law to an ecological catastrophe. The Temple Compact — divine authority filling the Senate's administrative void, with doctrinal disagreements about whether the gods planned any of this. The Kobold Clans — fractured: some building shrines to dead dragons, some trying to become what dragons were, some who've gone quiet and moved deeper and stopped talking to outsiders. The Lich Underground — post-Senate magicians outside the licensing system, who understood what the dragons were for and are not sharing that understanding freely. Goblin Mercenary Unions — organised, opinionated, contractually protected, and currently the most reliable source of information about what's actually coming up from underground.

The Rumour

There is a ward-line in the northern reaches that should have failed eighteen months ago. It hasn't. A kobold clan moved deeper and went silent — no mourning, no shrine-building, no doomsday preaching. Just gone quiet. Miners in the Ashfold Mountains say the ground still hums the old way. Every seer who tries to answer the question are they truly all gone? gets the same response: not the silence of absence. The silence of something choosing not to answer.

The rumour of a surviving dragon is itself a setting aspect — one that factions will kill over, betray over, build religions around. The PCs don't need to find a dragon to feel its pull. They just need to live in a world where the possibility changes what everyone does. Pay it off if you want. Leave it unresolved if you don't. The second choice is more Fate.
