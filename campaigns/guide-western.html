★ Dust and Iron

Frontier justice. Railroad money. The weight of the old war.

The survey stakes went in at dawn. The Harker Company wants the land. The homesteaders pulled the stakes up. The law rides for the company. The circuit judge is two days out and the verdict is already written. You're somewhere in between — which means everybody needs something from you and nobody's going to say thank you.

Tone & Voice

Hard-earned and spare. The frontier strips things down to what they actually are. People are loyal or they aren't. Land is worth what someone will kill for. The darkness in this setting comes from human choices — greed, cowardice, the specific cruelty of men with institutional backing. Violence has weight. Death is rarely poetic. The railroad is coming whether anyone wants it or not.

The territory is not lawless. It's worse: the law is present and it belongs to someone. Your characters are the people who aren't in that pocket — or who used to be and are trying to get out.

For Players

You ride with a past and a price. Every character has a debt, a reputation, and something they're trying to protect that is probably worth less than what it's going to cost them to protect it. The Troubles are the things that made you dangerous and the things that followed you here. The compels will find them. Use them first.

Key Factions

The Harker Company (land acquisition, bought law, corporate patience), the Cattlemen's Association (the old guard, fencing wars, diminishing leverage), the Railroad Trust (progress as inevitability, indifferent to what's in the way), homesteaders and small ranchers (the stakes are their lives and their children's lives), circuit judges and marshals (the law, such as it is, going where the money points), Pinkerton agents (hired trouble from back East, professional, thorough, loyal to the contract not the territory).

Inspirations

Blood Meridian, Deadwood, True Grit, Lonesome Dove, No Country for Old Men, Hell on Wheels, Tombstone, The Assassination of Jesse James

Running Fate on the Frontier

Gunfights are narrative, not tactical. Describe the cover, the light, the number of exchanges before someone's horse bolts. Stress is how long you stay in the fight — not how many bullets you absorb. A taken-out result is surrender, flight, or getting shot. Which one fits the fiction.

The law as opposition. The sheriff, the marshal, the Pinkerton — these are antagonists with professional standing and institutional backing. They can compel your aspects and invoke situation aspects against you. The Resources gap between the Harker Company and your players is the engine of every story in this setting.

The old war. The War Between the States ended on paper. Half the people on the frontier are running from what they did in it. Aspects and Troubles that reference the war give the GM free compels for life.
