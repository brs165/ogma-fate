The World

Dust and Iron is the frontier. The great cattle drives are ending. The railroad is coming. The old way of doing things — by gun, by handshake, by whoever shows up with more riders — is running out of road.

The territory is not lawless. It's worse: the law is present and it belongs to someone. The sheriff, the land deed, and the territorial judge are all instruments of the same pocket. Your characters are the people who aren't in that pocket — or who used to be.

Inspirations: Blood Meridian, Deadwood, True Grit, Lonesome Dove, No Country for Old Men.

Tone & Voice

Hard-earned and spare. Bone-dry. The frontier strips things down to what they are. People are loyal or they aren't. Land is worth what someone will kill for. The darkness comes from human choices. Violence has weight. Death is rarely poetic.

For Players

You ride with a past and a price. Every character has a debt, a reputation, and something they're trying to protect. The Troubles are the things that made you dangerous and the things that followed you here. Use them.

Key Factions

The Harker Company (land acquisition, bought law), the Cattlemen's Association (the old guard), the Railroad Trust (progress, inevitable, indifferent), homesteaders (the stakes are their lives), circuit judges and marshals (the law, such as it is), Pinkerton agents (hired trouble from back East).

Running Fate in the Frontier

Gunfights are narrative, not tactical. Describe the cover, the light, the number of exchanges before someone's horse starts. Stress is how long you can stay upright — not how many bullets you absorb.

The law as opposition. The sheriff, the marshal, the Pinkerton — these are antagonists with their own agenda. Compel player aspects with their presence.

Land and money. Resources covers land, credit, and who owes whom. The Harker Company has Resources: Fantastic. Your players probably have Resources: Fair at best. That gap is the engine of every story.
