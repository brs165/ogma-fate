Coming from D&D 5e?

Fate Condensed isn't better or worse than D&D - it's a genuinely different kind of game. This guide covers the conceptual shifts that trip up experienced D&D players and GMs, with side-by-side comparisons and concrete examples. Read it before your first session and you'll be ahead of the curve.

The Big Philosophical Shift

In D&D, you interact with a world the GM has built. The rules simulate physics - armor class, damage dice, movement speed - and the fiction follows the mechanics.

In Fate, the fiction comes first. You describe what you're trying to do, the table decides if it makes sense, and then you pick the mechanic that fits. The dice don't tell you what happens - they place constraints on the narration.

The Book of Hanz puts it perfectly: "Fiction, not physics." Fate doesn't simulate a world. It simulates a story about characters in a world.

D&D 5e

"I roll to attack. 17 hits? OK, I do 8 slashing damage."

Fate Condensed

"I lunge for his sword arm - I want to disarm him." "Sounds like Fight against his Athletics. Roll it."

Mindset Tip

The biggest adjustment isn't learning new rules - it's unlearning the habit of looking up what you're "allowed" to do. In Fate, if it makes sense in the fiction, you can try it. The rules resolve the outcome, they don't define the options.

Characters: Aspects vs. Ability Scores

A D&D character is defined by numbers: six ability scores, a class, hit points. A Fate character is defined by five aspects - short phrases that say who this person is, what drives them, and what gets them in trouble.

A Fate Character Has:

5 Aspects - High Concept, Trouble, Relationship, and two free aspects
Skill Pyramid - one Great (+4), two Good (+3), three Fair (+2), four Average (+1)
3 Stunts - narrow bonuses or rule-bending tricks
Stress - 3 physical + 3 mental boxes (plot armor, clears each scene)
Consequences - Mild (2 shifts), Moderate (4), Severe (6)
Refresh 3 - fate points you start each session with

No hit points, no spell slots, no passive perception, no alignment. Your personality, capabilities, and vulnerabilities are all in those five aspect phrases.

D&D: "Level 5 Human Fighter, Champion, STR 18, DEX 14, CON 16."

Fate: High Concept: Disgraced Knight of the Silver Order · Trouble: The Drink Takes What the War Left · Relationship: I Owe Mara a Debt I Can't Name

The D&D version tells you what the character can do. The Fate version tells you who they are - and every phrase comes up in play, both as a bonus and as a complication.

Skills: Broad Strokes, Not Specifics

Fate Condensed has 19 skills, each covering a broad area. No weapon proficiencies, no tool checks, no saving throws as a separate mechanic. Skills are arranged in a pyramid (+4, +3, +3, +2, +2, +2, +1, +1, +1, +1). You roll 4 Fate dice (each -1, 0, or +1) and add your skill. The bell curve trends heavily toward your rating.

D&D Habit to Break

In D&D, you say "I roll Perception." In Fate, describe what you're doing: "I scan the rooftops for snipers." The GM decides the skill: Notice (spotting in the moment) vs. Investigate (piecing together clues). Fiction first, skill second.

The key insight: skills represent what you're trying to accomplish, not a specific physical action. You don't roll Athletics to climb a wall - you roll Athletics to overcome an obstacle requiring physical effort. The wall is flavor.

The Four Actions

D&D has attacks, saves, ability checks, and a complex action economy. Fate has exactly four actions:

Overcome · Create Advantage · Attack · Defend

Overcome - remove an obstacle. Closest to a D&D ability check.
Create Advantage - introduce new aspects or add free invokes. D&D has no equivalent.
Attack - try to harm someone. Outcome is shifts, not damage dice.
Defend - resist an attack or advantage creation.

Each has four outcomes: Fail, Tie, Succeed, Succeed with Style (3+ shifts). Every outcome does something - even failure advances the story, usually at a cost.

Failure Is Interesting

In D&D, a missed attack means nothing happens. In Fate, the GM should make failure mean something: succeed at a serious cost, the situation worsens, or a new complication appears. The story never stalls on a bad roll.

Aspects: The Heart of the System

An aspect is a short phrase describing something important about a person, place, or situation. The critical rule: aspects are always true.

If a room is Pitch Black, you cannot see - not "you have disadvantage on Perception." If someone is Pinned Under the Wreckage, they can't walk away. Aspects define what's possible, not just what gets a bonus.

What Aspects Do

Grant permission - a Wizard of the Silver Tower can use magic. You can't.
Make you awesome - invoke for +2 or a reroll when relevant.
Get you in trouble - the GM compels them to create complications.
Define the scene - Crates of TNT and Howling Winds shape what's possible.
Be true - they don't need invocation to matter. They just are.

D&D

"Difficult terrain. Movement costs double." A mechanical rule, applied uniformly.

Fate

"The floor is Covered in Caustic Slime." Invoke it, compel it, use it for narrative permission, or have it restrict actions. It's a story element, not a rule lookup.

The "Aspects Are Feats" Trap

Coming from D&D, it's natural to think aspects are "feat-like bonuses you named yourself." They're not. An aspect is a true thing about the world. The +2 invoke is just one of its uses. The narrative truth is more powerful than the +2.

Fate Points: The Economy of Drama

D&D has Inspiration (which many tables forget). Fate has a core economy that drives every session.

How Fate Points Flow

Start each session with fate points = your Refresh (default 3).
Spend to invoke an aspect (+2 or reroll) or declare a story detail.
Earn when the GM compels your aspects, when you concede, or from hostile invocations.
Refuse a compel by spending a fate point - but at zero, you can't refuse.

This creates a rhythm: the more trouble you accept, the more power you accumulate. Accept your Trouble complicating scene 1, and you have extra fate points for the climax in scene 5.

The Compel Cycle: Your Trouble is The Drink Takes What the War Left. GM says: "You've had a few too many by the time the informant arrives - they don't take you seriously. Take a fate point?" You accept. Later, you invoke Disgraced Knight with that fate point when you need to impress the rebel leader.
D&D Habit to Break

In D&D, the goal is to minimize bad things happening. In Fate, you want bad things at the right time - that's how you earn fuel to be awesome later. Compels aren't punishment; they're the story getting more interesting and you getting paid for it.

The GM's pool: 1 fate point per PC per scene. Spent to invoke on behalf of NPCs. Compels are free and unlimited. The GM's job is to spend their budget creating drama.

Combat: Stress, Consequences, and Taken Out

This is where D&D players struggle most, because the words sound familiar but mean different things.

D&D 5e

Hit Points count down to 0. Damage = dice rolls subtracted from HP. 0 HP = unconscious + death saves. Healing = spells that restore numbers.

Fate Condensed

Stress boxes are binary, each absorbs 1 shift, clear every scene. Consequences are aspects on your sheet: Mild=2, Mod=4, Severe=6 shifts. Taken Out = opponent narrates your fate. Recovery = overcome roll + time.

Stress is not damage. It's plot armor - "I barely dodged" or "that knocked the wind out of me." Default is 3 physical + 3 mental boxes. Higher Physique or Will gives more.

Consequences are real injuries - and they're aspects. A Gaping Chest Wound is true. The enemy gets a free invoke. Anyone can compel it. It restricts what you can do.

Taking a 5-shift hit: 1 stress box left. Take a Moderate consequence (Gaping Chest Wound, absorbs 4) + last stress box (absorbs 1). 4 + 1 = 5. You survive - but now the enemy invokes that wound against you.
Conceding: The Escape Valve

Before any roll, you can concede. You lose, but you decide how you exit. You get a fate point for conceding plus one per consequence taken. Losing on your terms with resources for the next scene is often smarter than getting taken out.

Recovery: Stress clears at end of scene. Consequences need an overcome roll (Academics for physical, Empathy for mental), then: Mild clears after next scene, Moderate after a full session, Severe after a major milestone. Self-treatment adds +2 to difficulty.

Initiative: Popcorn Style

D&D rolls initiative once, producing a fixed order. Fate uses popcorn initiative: after you act, you pick who goes next - ally or enemy.

How It Works

1. The table decides who goes first (negotiated, not rolled).
2. That character acts.
3. They choose who goes next - anyone who hasn't gone this exchange.
4. After everyone goes, the last person picks who starts next exchange.
5. GM's NPCs are in the order just like PCs.

Why This Matters

In D&D, initiative is something that happens to you. In Fate, turn order is a resource you manage. Pass to your ally so they can use the advantage you just created. Or force the enemy to go before they're set up. Teams that coordinate will dominate.

Create Advantage: The Action D&D Doesn't Have

This is the most important tactical action in Fate, and D&D has no equivalent. Create Advantage introduces a new aspect or adds free invokes to an existing one.

Example: Instead of attacking the armored warlord (his Fight +6 vs. your Fight +4 - bad matchup), use Provoke to create Blinded by Rage. You get a free invoke. Pass to your ally, who attacks using Fight + your free invoke (+2). That +2 cost no fate point and came from a skill matchup you could win.

Why it's so powerful: It lets you use your best skills even in combat. A character with Provoke +4 is more useful taunting than swinging a mediocre sword. It generates free invokes (no FP cost). It creates narrative truth. And it lets the team stack bonuses.

The Create Advantage Cascade

The deadliest tactic in Fate: 2-3 characters spend an exchange Creating Advantages, piling up free invokes. Then the best attacker uses all of them on a single massive hit. A +4 attacker with three free invokes (+6) rolls at effective +10. That one hit often ends the fight. This is how Fate rewards teamwork over optimization.

Challenges, Contests, and Conflicts

D&D defaults nearly everything to combat. Fate has three scene types:

Challenge

A series of overcome actions against a dynamic situation with no "other side." Each player picks a task; GM considers the mix of results. Example: Escaping a collapsing ruin - navigate, hold a beam, disarm a trap, carry the wounded.

Contest

Two sides pursuing mutually exclusive goals, not trying to harm each other. Opposed overcome rolls per exchange; first to 3 victories wins. Ties cause twists. Example: A chase. A debate before the council. A race to decode the signal.

Conflict

Characters can and want to harm each other. Attack/defend rolls, stress and consequences, zones, popcorn initiative. Example: A swordfight. A shootout. A psychic assault. An interrogation inflicting mental consequences.

The Key Question

Can the characters harm each other? If yes → Conflict. Racing toward opposing goals? → Contest. Complex situation, no clear enemy? → Challenge. Many D&D combats are actually Contests or Challenges in Fate.

For the GM: Running Fate

You build situations, not encounters. Instead of CR budgets, set up interesting problems with 3-5 situation aspects, clear stakes, and at least two interesting outcomes.

You don't own the story. When a player Creates an Advantage declaring the guard's sword is Old and Brittle, that's true. When someone invokes an aspect to add a story detail - "there's a back alley" - and it makes sense, it exists.

Setting difficulty: Routine? Don't roll. Tough? Set opposition around their skill level. Nearly impossible? Go higher. Mediocre (+0) is trivial, Great (+4) is serious, Fantastic (+6) is extraordinary.

GM Fate Point Budget

1 fate point per PC per scene. Invoke on behalf of NPCs. Compels are free and unlimited. Spend your budget making scenes dangerous and interesting. Don't hoard.

NPCs are simpler. Minor NPCs: 1-2 aspects, a skill or two, 0-2 stress boxes - designed to be defeated. Major NPCs: full aspects, skill list, stress, consequences - session anchors. No monster manual needed.

The Hardest Adjustment

In D&D, pacing comes from encounter design. In Fate, pacing comes from the fate point economy. Story feels flat? Compel more. Players feel weak? They're not earning enough fate points through trouble. The economy is the engine.

Session Zero: Building Together

In D&D, you build characters independently. In Fate, character creation is collaborative - the group decides what the game is about together.

You establish setting aspects as a group. You create characters whose aspects connect through the Relationship aspect and Phase Trio. By the end, every character has a reason to be here and at least one tie to another PC.

Why This Matters

In D&D, "why is the party together?" is often hand-waved. In Fate, it's mechanical - the Relationship aspect can be invoked and compelled. The party isn't together because the DM said so; they're together because their stories are intertwined.

Quick FAQ

Where's the damage? How much does a sword do?

No damage dice. Shifts (your effort minus their defense) = "damage." A sword doesn't inherently do more than a fist - what matters is skill rating, invocations, and stunts. A stunt giving +2 to Fight with a named blade? That's your sword damage.

Can my character die?

Only if you're taken out and the group agrees death is on the table. You can always concede before a roll to exit on your terms. Death should be dramatic, not a random bad roll.

What about magic?

No spell system. Magic = aspects (a Pyromancer has permission to do fire things) + stunts (use Lore to attack with fire). No slots, no components, no separate subsystem.

What about equipment and loot?

Important items become aspects (My Father's Cursed Blade) or stunts. Everything else is assumed - fighters have weapons, thieves have lockpicks. No inventory tracking.

How does leveling up work?

Milestones, not XP. Minor (per session): swap skills, rename an aspect. Significant (per arc): add skill points or stunts. Major (big campaign beat): increase refresh or rewrite high concept. Characters change more than they power up.

Where's the battle grid?

No grid, no squares, no feet. Scenes use 2-4 zones - abstract areas like "the rooftop" and "the alley." Move one zone free; more if nothing's in your way. Zones have aspects. Sketch it on an index card.

When do I roll?

Only when there's both a chance of failure and an interesting consequence. Picking a lock on an unguarded door with no pressure? Just do it. Picking it while guards patrol? Roll Burglary to overcome.

Won't players abuse the looseness?

Fate is collaborative. The table (not just the GM) decides what's plausible. Aspects have to pass the "sniff test." The social contract is the balancing mechanism.
