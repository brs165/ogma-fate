🌱 I've Never Played a Tabletop RPG

Imagine four people around a table. One of them - the Game Master (GM) - describes a world: a rain-soaked town, a locked door, a nervous stranger. The other three describe characters who live in that world. When something uncertain happens ("I try to pick the lock"), you roll dice. The dice don't tell you what happens - they tell you how well it goes. Everything else is a conversation.

That's a tabletop RPG. No board. No winning. Just collaborative storytelling with dice to keep it honest.

What is Fate Condensed?

Fate is one of many RPG systems (like choosing between card games - they all use cards, but Poker and Bridge play very differently). What makes Fate special is that it cares more about who your character is than what they can do. Your character is described by short phrases called aspects - things like "Retired Detective Who Can't Stop Noticing Things" or "My Loyalty Will Get Me Killed Someday." Those phrases are always true in the story, and they're also the engine that drives the game.

What do I need?

Four Fate dice (or a free dice app), some tokens (pennies, poker chips - anything small), index cards for notes, and one person willing to be the GM. This generator suite handles the rest - it creates characters, scenes, and adventures so the GM doesn't have to start from scratch.

What does a session look like?

The GM describes a scene: "You're in the back room of a diner. The lights are dim. A woman you've never met is sitting at your usual table." You decide what your character does: "I sit down across from her and say nothing." The GM decides if that requires a roll: "She's sizing you up - roll your Rapport skill to see if you make a good impression." You roll, add your skill number, and the result tells you how it goes. The conversation continues from there.

A session lasts 2-3 hours. Most of it is talking, imagining, and making decisions. The dice come out maybe 15-20 times. The rest is pure storytelling.

⚔ I Play D&D 5e

You already know what a TTRPG is. You know what a GM does. You understand skills, dice, and turn-based conflict. Good - that foundation transfers. But Fate Condensed is a fundamentally different kind of game, and the habits that make you a good D&D player will actively mislead you here. The shift isn't mechanical - it's philosophical.

The Three Things That Trip Up D&D Players Most

1. You describe first, then pick the mechanic. In D&D you say "I roll Athletics." In Fate you say "I sprint across the rooftop and leap for the fire escape" - and the GM decides which skill fits. Fiction drives mechanics, not the other way around.

2. Failure is never boring. A missed attack in D&D means nothing happens. In Fate, the GM offers "success at a cost" - you succeed, but something goes wrong. The story always moves forward.

3. Getting in trouble makes you stronger. In D&D, you minimise bad outcomes. In Fate, you accept complications (compels) to earn fate points - the currency you spend to be awesome later. Embracing your character's flaws is the optimal strategy.

🎲 I Play Other RPGs - What Makes Fate Different

You know what a GM is, what dice do, and what a skill check means. You don't need the basics. Here's what makes Fate Condensed different from whatever system you're coming from, in five concepts:

1. The Aspect Economy

Every important truth is an aspect - a short phrase. Character aspects, situation aspects, consequences. They can be invoked (spend a fate point → +2 or reroll), compelled (accept a complication → earn a fate point), and they're always narratively true. If the room is Pitch Black, you cannot see. Period. This is the core innovation - everything else flows from it.

2. Four Actions, Four Outcomes

Overcome (clear an obstacle), Create Advantage (add aspects to the scene), Attack (harm someone), Defend (resist). Each produces Fail / Tie / Succeed / Succeed with Style. No action economy, no bonus actions, no reactions. One action per turn.

3. Fiction First

Describe what you're doing. The table decides which skill and action fit. Then roll. If you're coming from PbtA, this is familiar. If you're coming from GURPS or Savage Worlds, it's a significant shift - there are no "I use my Rapid Strike maneuver" declarations. The fiction generates the mechanic, not vice versa.

4. Stress Is Not Hit Points

Stress boxes are binary (checked/unchecked), each absorbs 1 shift, and they clear every scene. They're plot armor, not health. Consequences (Mild=2, Moderate=4, Severe=6) are the real injuries - they're aspects that persist and can be invoked against you.

5. The Fate Point Cycle

Start with Refresh (default 3). Spend to invoke aspects. Earn by accepting compels. The rhythm: accept trouble now → be awesome later. The economy creates narrative structure without the GM having to pace it manually.

🎭 Help Me Run Fate Better

You understand the rules. You've read the book. Now you need to make it work at the table. Here's the craft.

The Golden Rule of Scene Framing

Every scene needs a question. "Can the party get past the guards?" "Will the informant trust them?" If you can't state it in one sentence, the scene isn't focused enough. When the question is answered - yes, no, or "yes, but…" - the scene is over. Cut immediately. Don't let scenes drift.

Setting Difficulty Without a Table

Don't look up a DC chart. Ask: is this hard for this character? If it's routine, don't roll. If it's tough, set opposition at or slightly above their skill. If it's nearly impossible, go 2-4 higher. The adjective ladder (Mediocre +0 through Legendary +8) is a calibration tool, not a lookup table.

The Compel Rhythm

If the story feels flat, you're not compelling enough. Every PC's Trouble should trigger at least once per session. Every situation aspect should be compelled before the scene ends. If you're not handing out fate points, the players can't spend them, and the whole economy stalls. Compels are the accelerator pedal.

Fail Forward, Always

"Nothing happens" is never the result of a failed roll. Success at a cost is your best friend. "You pick the lock, but the alarm was on a separate circuit." "You convince the guard, but he'll remember your face." "You find the clue, but the person who hid it knows you found it." Every failure changes the situation.

The Create Advantage Cascade

The first time a player stacks three free invokes and drops a +10 hit, they'll never attack-every-round again. Don't explain this tactic - engineer the first encounter so it happens naturally. Give them an enemy with Fight +6 who is clearly too strong to hit head-on. The party will figure out that two rounds of Create Advantage + one massive attack is better than four rounds of mediocre attacks.

NPC Shorthand

Minor NPCs: one aspect, one skill, 0-2 stress. They exist to be defeated. Major NPCs: full aspects, skill pyramid, consequences. They anchor sessions. Spend your prep time on majors and let the generators handle the rest.

📋 Quick Reference

The Four Actions
Overcome - remove obstacles (Fail: cost / Tie: minor cost / Succeed / SWS: succeed + boost)
Create Advantage - add/discover aspects (Fail: enemy gets invoke / Tie: boost / Succeed: 1 free invoke / SWS: 2 free invokes)
Attack - deal stress (shifts = effort – defense)
Defend - resist attacks or advantages

Fate Points
Start at Refresh (3). Spend → invoke aspect (+2 or reroll). Earn → accept a compel. Refuse compel → spend 1 FP. At 0 FP → cannot refuse compels.

Stress & Consequences
Stress: 3 boxes (1-point each), clears end of scene. More at Physique/Will +1(4 boxes) or +3(6 boxes).
Consequences: Mild (absorbs 2 - treat first, then clears next scene), Moderate (absorbs 4 - treat first, then clears after one full session), Severe (absorbs 6 - treat first, then clears after a major milestone).
Treatment = overcome roll: Fair+2 mild, Great+4 moderate, Fantastic+6 severe. +2 difficulty self-treating. Clearing happens after treatment - not automatically.

Adjective Ladder
+8 Legendary · +7 Epic · +6 Fantastic · +5 Superb · +4 Great · +3 Good · +2 Fair · +1 Average · +0 Mediocre · -1 Poor · -2 Terrible

📚 Glossary

Aspects & Fate Points
Aspect
A short phrase that is always true in the fiction. Can be invoked for a bonus or compelled to create trouble.
High Concept
The aspect that defines who your character fundamentally is. The first thing to compel.
Trouble
The aspect that defines what gets your character into trouble. The GM's primary compel target.
Situation Aspect
An aspect attached to the scene, not a character. Lasts as long as the situation does.
Boost
A temporary, unnamed aspect with one free invoke. Cannot be compelled. Vanishes after use.
Fate Point (FP)
The game's currency. Spend to invoke aspects or declare story details. Earn through compels and concessions.
Invoke
Spend a fate point (or free invoke) to get +2 or a reroll on a dice roll related to an aspect.
Free Invoke
A one-time use of an aspect that doesn't cost a fate point. Earned through Create Advantage or by causing a consequence.
Hostile Invocation
Invoking an opponent's aspect against them. Costs a fate point - the target receives that FP after the scene.
Compel
An offer to complicate a character's life based on an aspect. Accept → take the fate point. Refuse → spend one.
Actions
Overcome
An action to get past an obstacle, remove a situation aspect, or accomplish a goal against opposition.
Create Advantage
An action that places a new aspect on the scene or a target, or discovers an existing hidden aspect, gaining free invokes.
Succeed with Style
Beating the opposition by 3+ shifts. Grants extra benefits: a boost, an extra free invoke, or a better outcome.
Shifts
The difference between your effort and the opposition total. Determines how well an action succeeds.
Conflict
Exchange
One round of a conflict. Every participant acts once via popcorn initiative, then a new exchange begins.
Popcorn Initiative
After you act, you choose who goes next - ally or enemy. No fixed turn order; creates tactical tension.
Concede
Voluntarily exit a conflict before being taken out. You lose, but you narrate how. Earn 1 FP + 1 per consequence taken in the conflict.
Taken Out
When you can't absorb a hit with stress or consequences. The attacker decides what happens to you.
Stress & Consequences
Stress
Temporary hit absorption. Each box absorbs 1 shift. All stress clears at the end of every scene.
Consequence
A lasting aspect taken to absorb a hit: mild (2 shifts), moderate (4), severe (6). Persists until treated and recovered.
Recovery (Mild)
Requires a successful overcome roll at Fair (+2) - Academics (physical) or Empathy (mental). Clears after one full scene. +2 difficulty if self-treating.
Recovery (Moderate)
Overcome at Great (+4). Clears after a full session once treatment succeeds. +2 if self-treating.
Recovery (Severe)
Overcome at Fantastic (+6). Clears after a major milestone once treatment succeeds. +2 if self-treating.
Character
Stunt
A special ability: +2 in a specific narrow circumstance, or a once-per-scene rule exception. Each stunt reduces refresh by 1.
Refresh
The minimum fate points you start each session with. Default 3, reduced by stunts (minimum 1).
Milestone (Minor)
End-of-session advancement. Swap a skill rank, rename an aspect, rewrite a stunt, or move a skill point. No power increase.
Milestone (Major)
End-of-arc advancement. Everything a minor milestone offers, plus increase one skill by one rank, and clear a severe consequence in recovery.
Scene & Setting
Zone
An abstract area of a scene. Move one zone free per exchange. Anyone in the same zone can interact directly.
Scale
An optional rule for conflicts between entities of very different size or power. Each scale step above is worth +1 to attack and defence.
Teamwork
Combine skills in a group action. The lead rolls; each helper with Average (+1) or better adds +1. Maximum bonus equals the lead's skill rank.
